850 Hz - Pure Tone for TMJ Relief & Sound Localization Support

14 Jun 2023

Temporomandibular Joint Disorders (TMJ) affect millions of people worldwide, causing pain, stiffness, and limited movement in the jaw joint and surrounding muscles. This condition can significantly impact daily life, making simple activities like eating, speaking, and even yawning uncomfortable or painful. While conventional treatments range from physical therapy and medications to dental interventions, many individuals seek complementary approaches that address both the physical discomfort and the underlying neurological factors involved in TMJ.

Enter 850 Hz, a pure tone frequency that has gained attention for its potential to offer relief for TMJ symptoms while also supporting auditory function. This frequency sits in the upper‑mid audible range and is thought to stimulate the auditory nerve and brainstem—key structures involved not only in hearing but also in regulating jaw muscles and pain perception. Additionally, 850 Hz may help with sound localization, the brain’s ability to identify where sounds are coming from in space.

In this article, we’ll explore how 850 Hz could benefit those with TMJ, the science behind its effects on the auditory system, its role in sound localization, and practical ways to incorporate this frequency into a holistic wellness routine.

Understanding Temporomandibular Joint Disorders (TMJ)

The temporomandibular joint (TMJ) is the hinge that connects your jawbone to your skull. It allows you to open and close your mouth, chew, speak, and make facial expressions. When this joint or the muscles that control it become inflamed, injured, or dysfunctional, a range of symptoms can occur, collectively referred to as temporomandibular disorders (TMD) or simply “TMJ.”

Common TMJ symptoms include:

  • Pain or tenderness in the jaw, face, neck, or shoulders
  • Clicking, popping, or grating sounds when moving the jaw
  • Limited jaw movement or locking of the jaw
  • Headaches, earaches, or ringing in the ears (tinnitus)
  • Difficulty chewing or a sudden uncomfortable bite

TMJ can arise from various factors, including jaw injury, arthritis, teeth grinding (bruxism), chronic stress, poor posture, or misalignment of the teeth or jaw. Because the condition involves both musculoskeletal and neurological components, effective management often requires a multi‑faceted approach.

How 850 Hz May Help with TMJ: The Auditory‑Jaw Connection

The proposed link between 850 Hz and TMJ relief centers on the anatomical and functional connections between the auditory system and the jaw. Here’s how this relationship works:

1. Stimulation of the Auditory Nerve

When you listen to an 850 Hz pure tone, the sound waves travel through the ear canal, vibrate the eardrum, and are transmitted via the ossicles to the cochlea. Inside the cochlea, hair cells convert the mechanical vibrations into electrical signals that travel along the auditory nerve (vestibulocochlear nerve) to the brainstem.

The auditory nerve carries not only hearing information but also interacts with other cranial nerves, including the trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V), which controls jaw movement and sensation. By stimulating the auditory nerve with a specific frequency like 850 Hz, it’s theorized that we may indirectly influence the trigeminal nerve, potentially helping to regulate jaw muscle tone and reduce spasms.

2. Brainstem Modulation

The brainstem is a critical hub where auditory signals are processed and relayed to higher brain centers. It also houses nuclei that control vital functions, including muscle coordination, pain perception, and autonomic responses.

Within the brainstem, the cochlear nuclei (auditory) and the trigeminal nuclei (jaw/facial) are in close proximity. Stimulating the auditory pathway with 850 Hz could create a neuromodulatory effect on nearby trigeminal circuits, potentially:

  • Reducing hypertonicity (excessive tension) in the jaw muscles
  • Modulating pain signals from the TMJ region
  • Promoting relaxation in the masticatory (chewing) muscles

This concept aligns with existing therapies like auditory integration training and certain forms of sound therapy, which use specific frequencies to influence neurological function beyond hearing.

3. Pain Perception and the Gate Control Theory

The gate control theory of pain suggests that non‑painful input (such as sound vibration) can “close the gate” to painful input, reducing the perception of pain. By providing a steady, non‑noxious auditory stimulus at 850 Hz, the brain may prioritize this input over TMJ‑related pain signals, offering temporary relief.

850 Hz and Sound Localization

In addition to its potential benefits for TMJ, 850 Hz is noted for supporting sound localization—the ability to determine where a sound is coming from in three‑dimensional space. This skill relies on precise timing and intensity differences between the sounds reaching each ear, processed by brainstem structures like the superior olivary complex.

Why Sound Localization Matters

Sound localization is essential for:

  • Spatial awareness and navigation
  • Focusing attention on relevant sounds in noisy environments
  • Communication and social interaction
  • Safety (e.g., identifying the direction of approaching vehicles)

Frequencies around 850 Hz fall within a range where the human auditory system is particularly sensitive to interaural time and level differences, making them effective for training or supporting localization abilities.

How 850 Hz May Enhance Sound Localization

Listening to an 850 Hz pure tone—especially through stereo headphones—provides a consistent, bilateral stimulus that can help “calibrate” the auditory pathways responsible for localization. Regular exposure may:

  • Strengthen neural connections in the brainstem auditory nuclei
  • Improve binaural processing (how the brain integrates input from both ears)
  • Enhance auditory attention and discrimination

For individuals with TMJ, improved sound localization may be a secondary benefit that contributes to overall auditory wellness, especially if TMJ symptoms are accompanied by ear‑related issues like tinnitus or ear fullness.

Potential Benefits of 850 Hz

While scientific research specifically on 850 Hz is limited, the theoretical framework and anecdotal reports suggest several potential benefits:

1. TMJ Symptom Relief

  • Reduced jaw tension and pain
  • Decreased frequency of jaw clicking or locking
  • Improved range of motion in the jaw
  • Relief from associated headaches or earaches

2. Auditory and Neurological Support

  • Enhanced sound localization and spatial hearing
  • Stimulation of auditory nerve and brainstem pathways
  • Potential support for tinnitus or hyperacusis (sound sensitivity)
  • Improved auditory processing and attention

3. Relaxation and Stress Reduction

  • Calming effect on the nervous system
  • Reduction of stress‑related jaw clenching
  • Promotion of overall relaxation, which can indirectly benefit TMJ

How to Use the 850 Hz Pure Tone

Incorporating 850 Hz into your routine is simple and requires minimal equipment. Here’s a step‑by‑step guide to get started.

What You’ll Need

  • 850 Hz Audio Source: This can be a pure‑tone generator app, a YouTube video, an audio file (e.g., MP3/WAV), or a dedicated frequency‑generating device.
  • Headphones or Speakers: For personal listening, stereo headphones are recommended to deliver the tone evenly to both ears. For ambient use, speakers can fill the room.
  • Comfortable Environment: Choose a quiet space where you can sit or lie down without interruptions for 10–20 minutes.

Suggested Protocol

  1. Set Your Intention: Before starting, clarify your goal—whether it’s TMJ relief, auditory training, or general relaxation.
  2. Get Comfortable: Sit or lie in a relaxed position. If focusing on TMJ, gently relax your jaw, allowing your teeth to separate slightly and your tongue to rest on the roof of your mouth.
  3. Start the Audio: Begin playing the 850 Hz tone at a comfortable volume—audible but not overwhelming.
  4. Passive Listening: Simply listen to the tone. There’s no need to concentrate intensely. You may focus on the sound, or let your mind wander while the frequency does its work.
  5. Duration: A typical session lasts 10–30 minutes. For TMJ support, you might listen 1–3 times daily, especially during periods of increased jaw tension or pain.
  6. Close the Session: Gradually reduce the volume and take a few deep breaths. Gently move your jaw in small circles or open/close movements to assess any changes in comfort.

Tips for Enhanced Results

  • Combine with Jaw Relaxation Exercises: While listening, practice gentle jaw stretches or massage the temporalis and masseter muscles.
  • Use During Stressful Activities: Play 850 Hz softly in the background during work, driving, or other activities that tend to increase jaw clenching.
  • Pair with Heat Therapy: Apply a warm compress to your jaw area before or during the listening session to enhance muscle relaxation.
  • Consistency is Key: Regular use over days or weeks may yield more noticeable benefits than occasional sessions.

Can 850 Hz Be Combined with Other Frequencies?

Yes, 850 Hz can be combined with other frequencies, especially if you’re addressing multiple concerns. Some combinations to consider:

  • 850 Hz + 20 Hz: For pain relief and deeper relaxation
  • 850 Hz + 432 Hz: For overall calming and stress reduction

Conclusion: A Sonic Approach to Jaw Comfort and Auditory Clarity

850 Hz presents a unique intersection of auditory stimulation and potential musculoskeletal benefits. By targeting the auditory nerve and brainstem—structures intimately connected to jaw function and pain perception—this frequency offers a non‑invasive, accessible option for those exploring complementary TMJ support.

Whether you’re dealing with jaw tension, seeking to enhance your sound localization abilities, or simply curious about the therapeutic potential of pure tones, 850 Hz is worth experimenting with.
